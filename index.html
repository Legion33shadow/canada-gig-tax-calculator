Frequently Asked Questions

Why do I pay double CPP as a self-employed person?

When you're employed, your employer pays 50% of CPP and you pay the other 50%. As a self-employed gig worker, you pay BOTH halves — currently 11.9% of net income up to the YMPE ($73,200 in 2026). This is one of the biggest hidden costs of freelancing in Canada.

What is the GST/HST $30,000 threshold exactly?

Once your total worldwide taxable supplies (revenue) exceed $30,000 over 4 consecutive quarters or in a single quarter, you MUST register for GST/HST within 29 days. You then charge GST/HST to clients and remit it to CRA quarterly or annually.

Can I deduct home office expenses as a gig worker?

Yes. If you use part of your home exclusively for work, you can deduct a proportional share of rent, utilities, internet, and maintenance. Typical deduction: 10–25% of housing costs, depending on space used.

Do I need to pay taxes in instalments?

Yes, if your net tax owing exceeds $3,000 (or $1,800 in Quebec), CRA requires quarterly instalment payments: March 15, June 15, September 15, December 15. Missing these triggers instalment interest charges.

Is EI worth it for self-employed Canadians?

Self-employed EI is optional and covers maternity/parental and sickness benefits only (not regular unemployment). Premium in 2026: ~$1.66 per $100 of insurable earnings. Most gig workers skip it unless planning parental leave.

How accurate is this calculator?

This calculator uses 2026 federal and provincial tax brackets, CPP rates (employee + employer = 11.9% up to YMPE), and standard BPA deductions. It is accurate for straightforward self-employment income. Complex situations (RRSP, capital gains, HST ITCs) require a CPA.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do I pay double CPP as a gig worker?

Employed Canadians split CPP 50/50 with their employer. As a self-employed worker, you are your own employer — you pay both halves. In 2026: 11.9% of net self-employment income above $3,500, up to maximum $73,200. On $60,000 net income, that’s approximately $7,100 in CPP alone — vs ~$3,550 for a salaried employee earning the same.

What is the GST/HST $30,000 threshold?

Once your worldwide taxable supplies exceed $30,000 in any single quarter or over four consecutive calendar quarters, you must register within 29 days. Failing to register = CRA assesses GST on your past revenue (you absorb the cost), plus penalties and compounded interest. After registering you can claim Input Tax Credits (ITCs) on all business purchases.

Can I deduct home office costs?

Yes — for self-employed individuals, if the space is used exclusively and regularly for work. Calculate the percentage by square footage. Deductible: proportional rent/mortgage interest, utilities, internet, property taxes. Keep monthly receipts. CRA digital audits match T1 deductions against bank statements.

When do I need to pay instalments?

If your net tax owing exceeds $3,000 ($1,800 in Quebec) for both the current year and either of the two prior years, CRA requires quarterly instalments: March 15 · June 15 · September 15 · December 15. Missing instalments triggers 9% annualized prescribed-rate interest in 2026. Set up an auto-transfer on those dates to avoid surprises.

Is incorporating worth it for gig workers?

Generally yes if net income consistently exceeds $80,000–$100,000. A CCPC pays 9% federal small business rate on first $500,000 of active income vs your marginal personal rate (up to 33% federal + provincial). The main advantage is income deferral: leave retained earnings in the corporation and only withdraw what you need personally. Incorporation cost: $1,500–$3,000. Most gig workers break even within 12 months at higher income levels.
